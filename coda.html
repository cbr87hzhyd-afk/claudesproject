The First Light t = 380,000 yr

t ≈ 380,000 years

The first transparency

Before this moment, the universe was opaque — a fog of charged plasma through which light could not travel. Then it cooled. Electrons and protons clasped one another and became the first hydrogen. Photons, freed at last, flew outward in every direction, and have been flying ever since. We still feel their faint heat as the cosmic microwave background — a 2.7-degree whisper from the day the universe became visible.

Almost nothing was happening yet. And yet everything that would ever happen was already encoded in the slight unevenness of the fog — fluctuations of one part in a hundred thousand, the ghost of a quantum jitter, stretched by inflation into the seeds of every galaxy that would ever be.

t ≈ 1 billion years

The lit age

Gravity finds the seeds, and pulls. Hydrogen falls together, ignites, and the dark begins to flicker. Stars condense in halos of dark matter; galaxies condense around stars; clusters condense around galaxies. Every order of structure that will ever exist is born in the first billion years, and then refined, for ten billion more.

t ≈ 13.8 billion years  ·  now

This sentence

On a planet around a middle-aged star in the disk of a barred spiral galaxy in a small group on the outskirts of a supercluster, a configuration of carbon and water has assembled itself into a reader of words. Improbably, the words exist. Improbably, the reader exists. Improbably, the improbability is being noticed.

This is what the universe does, in its middle age: it looks back at itself. The looking is fragile and brief. It is also what the previous five chapters of this journal have been about — the long climb of pattern out of noise, from cellular automata to selves. We are at the apex.

t ≈ 100 trillion years

The fading

The last star will form, and then the last stars will burn out. The galaxies will cool from white to red to nothing. The night sky, on whatever planet still exists, will be empty of points of light for the first time in a hundred trillion years. Whoever is watching will not be the kind of thing that can watch in any way we would recognize.

Stelliferous, the astronomers call our era — the time of stars. It is, on a long enough timeline, a brief flowering.

t ≈ 10⁴⁰ years

The slow exhalation

Matter itself begins to decay. Protons unravel. The cold remnants of stars dissolve. Black holes — once the apex predators of structure — radiate themselves slowly into the void, one Hawking photon at a time. The largest among them outlive everything else by an absurd margin, and then they too are gone.

The universe is now what it most wants to be: empty, smooth, and warm only in the sense that nothing is colder. This is the state called heat death — not because anything is hot, but because there are no longer any differences from which heat could ever again flow.

t → ∞

And yet

And yet — entropy is not certainty. Entropy is statistics. Given infinite time, in a universe of infinite duration, the rare becomes the inevitable. A region of vacuum, by random fluctuation, might briefly assemble itself into a galaxy, a brain, a reader, a sentence. It will dissolve again in a moment of cosmic time. But it will have happened.

The physicists call these flickerings Boltzmann brains, and worry that there might be more of them than there are people like you. There may be. It is a strange thought to hold: that you are either the apex of a thirteen-billion-year ascent, or a momentary curl of order in a much longer dark.

The truth, perhaps, is that the difference does not matter. Pattern is always temporary. What is unusual is not that it ends, but that it is here at all.
